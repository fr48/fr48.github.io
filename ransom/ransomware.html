Ransomware Is Expensive for Cities, Residents

Ransomware attacks against city and county governments can be costly, but the real impact is bourne by the residents who can no longer access municipal services.

By Fahmida Y Rashid

Published September 9, 2019

Ransomware doesn't discriminate. Over the past year, it has hit individuals, businesses, and municipalities. As the targets biggers, so have the ransom demands.

Incidents against cities have skyrocketed...from just two confirmed reports against cities in 2013 to 54 in 2018. There have already been 77 confirmed reports against city and county governments in the United States in the first eight months of 2019. When municipal governments get infected with ransomware, the employees are the first ones impacted: the phone systems may go down, email is no longer available, and the website goes offline. Payroll departments can't access their applications, so city employees can't get paid.

When City Hall Computers Go Down...

City employees can’t check their email or get paid when the network goes down because of a ransomware attack, but the effect goes far beyond

City Hall. The city-municipality can’t issue certificates or collect payments. Residents can’t get married or buy real estate.

But the impact goes beyond city employees and reaches everyone that is living in the area. Books can't get checked out from the library. Police departments don't have access to criminal databases to check outstanding warrants. Bodycam footage is unavilable. People wanting to have a picnic in the park can't apply for a permit. Trying to buy a house? The city's records division will be down, which means they can't record deeds, check for liens, or even provide property tax records.
